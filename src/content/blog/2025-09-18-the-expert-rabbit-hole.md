One of the things I underestimated when becoming a Director of Engineering was how difficult it would be to stay out of topics I knew well. Most people told me it would be hard to stop coding. But there were so many organizational topics where implementing solutions myself was not feasible anyway.

The real challenge for me was resisting the temptation to use deep expertise. When discussions became technical, I could usually follow them without much effort. If a migration was stuck, a dataset was poorly documented, or teams disagreed about an implementation, I often caught myself thinking: *I'll just have a quick look.* That quick look rarely stayed quick.

I would open repositories, trace dependencies, sketch architectures, and slowly build my own understanding of the problem. I genuinely enjoyed it. I like figuring things out. I like digging into messy systems, structuring the issues, and proposing solutions. I caught myself doing this for minor services.

But the deeper I went, the less I was doing my actual job. Instead of helping the team think through the problem, I started thinking through it for them. Instead of creating the conditions for good decisions, I became part of making them even though the team probably still knew better what they were doing.

This pattern rarely happened in areas where I was not an expert. If a Staff Engineer explained a complex backend topic outside my expertise, I naturally stayed at the right altitude. I asked questions. I challenged assumptions. I tried to understand the trade-offs and impediments without pretending I knew more than the people closest to the problem. It was only in my comfort zone that I started drifting into execution.

I think this is one of the traps of senior leadership.

**The better you are at something, the harder it is to lead instead of doing.**

Expertise creates confidence, but it also creates temptation. Solving the problem yourself often feels faster. It feels productive. It is also satisfying. You get immediate feedback and the comforting feeling of making progress. Coaching someone else through the problem takes longer. Delegating requires patience. Asking questions instead of providing answers can feel inefficient, especially when you think you already know the answer. But that is exactly where the leverage is.

A director's job is not to solve the hardest technical problems in the organization. It is to help build an organization that can solve those problems without depending on the director.

I still go deep when I need to. Sometimes I need enough context to evaluate a proposal or understand whether a risk is real. Technical curiosity is part of the job. The difference is that I now try to be much more deliberate about how I spend my time. Before opening the repository, I ask myself:

**What decision am I trying to make? Is it more important than anything else?**

If I cannot answer that, there is a good chance I am entering the expert rabbit hole. I suspect every leader has their own version of it. It does not have to be technical. Whatever got you promoted is often the thing that is hardest to let go of. Becoming a better leader often means using your greatest technical strengths a little less.
