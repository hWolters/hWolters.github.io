One of the concepts that has influenced my leadership the most is psychological safety. The research is compelling. Psychological safety supports learning behaviours such as admitting mistakes, asking for help, challenging ideas, and voicing concerns. Amy Edmondson helped establish the modern, team-level concept, defining it as *"a shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk taking."*

In my first year as a Director of Engineering, I realized there is a misconception: Psychological safety does **not** mean work should feel comfortable.

## Safe does not mean easy

Leadership is full of uncomfortable moments: Someone receives difficult feedback, a team gets dissolved, a project is cancelled, an organizational change affects people's role, someone disagrees with a decision. Psychological safety can coexist with discomfort. It does not require leaders to remove every difficult feeling.

If every difficult conversation is avoided in the name of psychological safety, we don't end up with healthy teams. We end up with unresolved problems.

## Disagreement is not a breach of trust

One experience particularly challenged my thinking. A team strongly disagreed with an organizational decision. Different perspectives usually lead to better outcomes. What surprised me was how the discussion evolved. Instead of saying,

> "I disagree with this decision."

the conversation slowly became,

> "This has broken trust."

Those statements are fundamentally different. The first invites a discussion about the decision. The second calls for a different conversation: What commitment, process, or behaviour made this feel like a breach of trust? It made me wonder whether we sometimes use *trust* as shorthand for *I don't agree with this decision.*

That doesn't mean people can't be genuinely disappointed. Some decisions are painful. Some feel unfair from an individual's perspective. And sometimes a decision really does damage trust, especially when it breaks a commitment, is made without a clear process or is applied inconsistently. But disagreement alone is not evidence that trust has been broken.

## Trust and psychological safety are different

The literature actually distinguishes between trust and psychological safety.

Trust is often about another person. I trust that you are competent. I trust that your intentions are good. I trust that you will do what you said you would. It can also be about whether I trust an organisation's processes and commitments.

Psychological safety is about the environment. It's the shared belief that I can speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, or disagree without being punished or humiliated.

The two reinforce each other, but they are not the same thing. I can trust my manager. I can feel completely safe disagreeing with them. And I can still dislike the decision they make. 

## The real test of psychological safety

Psychological safety is not tested when everyone agrees. It is tested when they don't.

- Can people challenge each other without becoming personal?

- Can they lose an argument without withdrawing?

- Can they state their remaining concerns without penalty, even after a decision has been made?

- Can they continue treating each other with respect after conflict?

I remember one situation where a team told me consistently they felt psychologically safe and trusted each other. When one person decided to leave the team, they treated this person in a way I would describe as bullying. A team isn't psychologically safe simply because people are vocal about it. A team can be outwardly polite and still be unsafe: people may self-censor because promotions, belonging, or access to leadership are at risk. Safety is about the expected consequences of speaking up.

## Psychological safety requires accountability

I also started thinking about another misconception. Sometimes psychological safety gets interpreted as protecting people from uncomfortable situations or as lowering standards.

Giving difficult feedback is uncomfortable.

Receiving difficult feedback is uncomfortable.

Making unpopular decisions is uncomfortable.

Healthy disagreement is uncomfortable.

Leadership is uncomfortable.

Psychological safety does not mean protecting people from difficult conversations, receiving difficult feedback... It means making those conversations possible while holding people to clear standards and commitments.

## What I believe today

Psychological safety does not promise comfort, agreement, or immunity from consequences. It promises that people can take interpersonal risks: raise concerns, challenge a decision, admit an error or say "this feels unfair" without humiliation or retaliation. The leader's job is to distinguish productive discomfort from avoidable harm and to respond to both honestly.

The psychologically safest teams I have worked with often had the most difficult conversations early and would not even label themselves as a "psychologically safe" team.
