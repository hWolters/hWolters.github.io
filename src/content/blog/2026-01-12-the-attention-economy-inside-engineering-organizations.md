One of the first things you learn about productivity is the Eisenhower Matrix. Separate the urgent from the important. Spend time on important work before it becomes urgent. It is one of those frameworks that feels almost too obvious to question, and I still believe it is one of the most useful mental models for prioritization.

I started wondering why it became so much harder to actually follow.

As a leader, your attention becomes a shared resource that many other people depend on. Someone from the C-Level needs input for tomorrow's presentation. A Tech Lead needs advice on a difficult situation. Product wants to discuss roadmap trade-offs. Slack fills up with questions that each take only a few minutes but collectively consume the day.

None of these requests are unreasonable. Most of them are exactly the conversations a leader should have. The challenge is that they all compete for the same finite resource: your attention.

## Organizations naturally produce reactive work

We often talk about budgets, cloud costs, and engineering capacity because they are easy to measure. Attention is different. It does not appear in quarterly reports, cannot be translated into euros, and nobody can allocate more of it halfway through the quarter. Yet every meeting, interruption, document review, Slack notification, and escalation consumes a small part of that limited budget.

Organizations naturally generate reactive work. Nobody has to plan for those things to appear. They somehow do.

Reactive work almost always has a sender. A hiring decision comes with a recruiter explaining that the candidate has another offer. A conflict between teams comes with frustrated people asking you to intervene. An executive asks for input before tomorrow's leadership meeting. There is always someone waiting for an answer, which naturally creates urgency.

The work that improves the system rarely has a sender: redesigning ownership, developing leaders, improving architecture, creating better decision processes, or writing a diagnosis before proposing solutions. It is usually important but rarely urgent, and unlike reactive work, it almost never arrives on its own.

Nobody schedules a meeting titled *"Revisit whether our organizational structure still matches the problems we're trying to solve."* It is not pressing, and it only enters your calendar because you decide to make space for it.

## Strategy needs deliberate room

Important but not urgent work rarely happens because people agree that it is important. It happens because leaders deliberately create structures and room that protect it.

One experience made this particularly clear. Together with our Staff Engineers, we facilitated an engineering-wide Eisenhower Matrix. We identified the biggest technical risks, discussed organizational bottlenecks, and aligned on strategic initiatives that deserved attention before they became emergencies. The workshop felt successful because everyone agreed on what mattered most.

The following Monday, my calendar looked exactly as before.

Interviews. One-on-ones. Roadmap discussions. Escalations. Slack.

Understanding what was important had not created any additional capacity to actually work on it.

Migration weeks work because they reserve capacity for infrastructure work that otherwise keeps slipping into the future. Architecture reviews work because they create recurring opportunities to discuss long-term technical direction before problems become crises. Strategy offsites work because they create enough distance from day-to-day operations for people to think beyond the next sprint.

These mechanisms simply make it much harder for the topics to disappear.

I blocked recurring focus sessions in my calendar and treated them almost like leadership meetings. Unless they conflicted with a C-Level discussion or an important leadership round, they stayed exactly where they were. If I had to move one, I immediately rescheduled it. I would not simply skip it.

Declining meetings can easily feel like you are not being collaborative, especially when every invitation has a legitimate purpose. However, nobody else is going to protect this time for me. If I did not, more long-term work simply disappeared underneath the weight of day-to-day operations.

## Leadership is managing the tension

Organizations naturally produce reactive work.

Left alone, every calendar slowly fills with meetings, approvals, interviews, Slack messages, escalations, and small decisions that each seem perfectly reasonable in isolation.

Leadership is managing the tension between reactive work and strategic work.

Reactive work keeps the current organization running.

Strategic work changes how the organization runs.

Reactive work solves today's problems.

Strategic work makes tomorrow's problems easier to solve.

Both are necessary, but only reactive work appears automatically.
