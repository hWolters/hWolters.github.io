On Friday, we had aligned on the most important engineering risks. On Monday morning, none of them existed in my calendar. Together with our Staff Engineers, we had facilitated an engineering-wide Eisenhower Matrix. We identified technical risks, discussed organizational bottlenecks, and aligned on strategic initiatives that deserved attention before they became emergencies. The workshop felt useful because everyone agreed on what mattered most.

But understanding what mattered had not created any additional capacity to work on it. The Eisenhower Matrix is one of the first things you learn about productivity. Separate the urgent from the important. Spend time on important work before it becomes urgent. It is one of those frameworks that feels almost too obvious to question, and I still believe it is one of the most useful mental models for prioritization.

I started wondering why it became so much harder to actually follow. As a leader, your attention becomes a shared resource that many other people depend on. Someone from the C-Level needs input for tomorrow's presentation. A Tech Lead needs advice on a difficult situation. Product wants to discuss roadmap trade-offs. Slack fills up with questions that each take only a few minutes but collectively consume the day.

None of these requests are unreasonable and are exactly the conversations a leader should have. The challenge is that they all compete for the same finite resource: your attention. The problem is that organizations are much better at creating urgency for reactive work than for strategic work.

Every organization has an internal attention economy. Some topics naturally attract attention because someone is asking, waiting, escalating, or depending on an answer. Other topics only receive attention when someone deliberately protects space for them.

## Organizations naturally produce reactive work

We often talk about budgets, cloud costs, and engineering capacity because they are easy to measure. Attention is different. It does not appear in quarterly reports, cannot be translated into euros, and nobody can allocate more of it halfway through the quarter. Yet every meeting, interruption, document review, Slack notification, and escalation consumes a small part of that limited budget.

Organizations naturally generate reactive work. Nobody has to plan for those things to appear. They somehow do.

Reactive work almost always has a sender. A hiring decision comes with a recruiter explaining that the candidate has another offer. A conflict between teams comes with frustrated people asking you to intervene. An executive asks for input before tomorrow's leadership meeting. There is always someone waiting for an answer, which naturally creates urgency.

The work that improves the system rarely has a sender: redesigning ownership, developing leaders, improving architecture, creating better decision processes, or writing a diagnosis before proposing solutions. It is usually important but rarely urgent, and unlike reactive work, it almost never arrives on its own.

Nobody schedules a meeting titled *"Revisit whether our organizational structure still matches the problems we're trying to solve."* It is not pressing, and it only enters your calendar because you decide to make space for it.

That is what makes reactive work so powerful. It arrives with a person waiting. Strategic work often arrives as a vague sense that something will get worse if nobody makes time for it. The cost is rarely visible as one missed decision. It shows up later as the same escalation repeating, the same ownership question resurfacing, the same architectural risk appearing in every planning cycle or the same people becoming bottlenecks because nobody had time to change how decisions are made.

## Strategy needs deliberate room

Important but not urgent work rarely happens because people agree that it is important. It happens because leaders deliberately create structures that protect it.

After the workshop, my calendar looked exactly as before. Interviews. One-on-ones. Roadmap discussions. Escalations. Slack.

Migration weeks work because they reserve capacity for infrastructure work that otherwise keeps slipping into the future. Architecture reviews work because they create recurring opportunities to discuss long-term technical direction before problems become crises. Strategy offsites work because they create enough distance from day-to-day operations for people to think beyond the next sprint.

These mechanisms simply make it much harder for the topics to disappear. In a way, they give strategic work a sender. A recurring forum. A named owner. A document that has to be updated. A decision that needs to be revisited. A rhythm that keeps the topic alive when day-to-day work would otherwise push it aside.

That distinction matters because protecting attention cannot only be a personal productivity exercise. Blocking focus time helps, but it is not enough if the organization has no mechanism for the work to enter the system. Strategic work needs somewhere to live before it becomes an emergency.

I blocked recurring focus sessions in my calendar and treated them almost like leadership meetings. Unless they conflicted with a C-Level discussion or an important leadership round, they stayed exactly where they were. If I had to move one, I immediately rescheduled it. I would not simply skip it.

Declining meetings can easily feel like you are not being collaborative, especially when every invitation has a legitimate purpose. However, nobody else is going to protect this time for me. If I did not, more long-term work simply disappeared underneath the weight of day-to-day operations.

At the same time, reactive work is not the enemy. Helping a Tech Lead think through a difficult situation is leadership. Supporting Product on a roadmap trade-off is leadership. Giving input before an executive meeting can be exactly the right use of time. The issue is if reactive work becomes the default allocation mechanism for leadership attention. If the calendar is only shaped by who asks most urgently, it will not reliably reflect what matters most.

## Leadership is managing the tension

Organizations naturally produce reactive work. Left alone, every calendar slowly fills with meetings, approvals, interviews, Slack messages, escalations, and small decisions that each seem perfectly reasonable in isolation but miss the big picture. Leadership is finding the balance between reactive work and strategic work.

Reactive work keeps the current organization running.

Strategic work changes how the organization runs.

Reactive work solves today's problems.

Strategic work makes tomorrow's problems easier to solve.

Both are necessary, but only reactive work appears automatically. If strategic work only exists when nobody interrupts you, it will lose. The work that changes the system needs a place to live before the system asks for it.
